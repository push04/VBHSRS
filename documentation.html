1. Project Overview

The High Speed Rolling Stock (HSRS) Analysis Platform is a browser-based, real-time physics simulation and engineering analysis tool for high-speed passenger rail systems. It models traction, braking, wear, vibration, thermal behaviour, and ride comfort - entirely in-browser with no server dependency.

The platform was developed to support engineering analysis of high-speed rail (HSR) operations in India, particularly the transition from existing semi-HSR stock (160 km/h) to dedicated HSR rolling stock capable of 320 km/h on the Mumbai–Ahmedabad High Speed Rail corridor (508 km, MAHSR).

What Does It Simulate?

  • Real-time simulation loop - physics updated every 100 ms using Runge-Kutta 4th order (RK4) integration
  • Traction & braking - tractive effort curves, adhesion limits, braking distance
  • Rail-wheel tribology - contact stress, creep forces, wear volume (Hertz + Polach + Archard)
  • Suspension dynamics - frequency response, Sperling ride index, ISO 2631-1
  • Vibration analysis - vertical, lateral, jerk, yaw, roll with live charts
  • Thermal models - brake disc temperature, bearing temperature, hotbox detection
  • Energy consumption - Davis resistance, specific energy at speed
  • MAHSR corridor - ATC speed profiles, station geometry, journey time analysis

2. Glossary - Terms & Symbols Used Throughout

This section exists because most of the formulas below reuse the same handful of symbols and pieces of jargon over and over. Rather than re-explaining "creepage" every time it shows up, it's defined once here. Skim it once, then use it as a lookup when a symbol in a later formula box is unfamiliar.

2.1 Core Physics Terms

TermMeaning
Adhesion (µ)The friction-like coefficient that limits how much tractive or braking force a steel wheel can transmit to a steel rail before it slips. Unlike rubber-on-tarmac friction, wheel-rail adhesion is low (typically 0.1–0.4) and drops as speed rises - this single fact governs almost every traction and braking limit in the platform.
Creep / Creepage (ξ)Even when a wheel is not outright slipping, the contact patch is elastic: part of it sticks to the rail, part of it micro-slides. The overall mismatch between the wheel's surface speed and the rail's surface speed, expressed as a fraction, is the creepage. A creepage of 0.003 (0.3%) means the wheel surface is moving 0.3% faster or slower than the rail under it. That's imperceptible to a passenger, yet it is exactly this micro-slip that generates the tractive force that accelerates the train.
Contact patchThe small, roughly elliptical area (typically 60–90 mm², about the size of a large coin) where the curved wheel tread actually touches the curved rail head. The entire weight of a rail vehicle car (30–50 tonnes) passes through a handful of these coin-sized patches.
Hertzian contact stress (p₀)The peak pressure at the centre of the contact patch, from Heinrich Hertz's 1882 elastic contact theory. Because the contact patch is small, this pressure is enormous - typically 800–1600 MPa, roughly the same order of magnitude as the pressure inside a rifle barrel at the moment of firing.
Conicity (λ or γ)How tapered (cone-shaped, rather than cylindrical) the wheel tread profile is. A perfectly cylindrical wheel would run dead straight but couldn't self-steer around curves or recentre itself on straight track; a coned wheel can, but too much taper makes the wheelset dynamically unstable (see Hunting below).
HuntingA self-excited lateral (side-to-side) oscillation of a wheelset or bogie that appears above a critical speed, caused by the same conicity that lets the wheelset self-steer. Below critical speed it is a gentle, damped sway; above it, the oscillation grows until the wheel flange bangs into the rail repeatedly.
ShakedownThe material-mechanics threshold below which repeated rolling contact loading produces only elastic (fully reversible) deformation. Above it, each pass leaves a small amount of permanent plastic strain behind - this accumulates over millions of wheel passes into rolling contact fatigue cracks.
RatchettingThe progressive, one-directional accumulation of plastic strain when contact stress is pushed well above the shakedown limit - each rolling pass "ratchets" the material a little further, eventually causing surface cracking or material flow (lipping) at the rail gauge corner.
Rolling Contact Fatigue (RCF)The family of surface and near-surface cracking defects (squats, head checks, gauge-corner cracking, shelling) caused by cyclic rolling contact stress exceeding the material's fatigue or shakedown limit. It is the dominant rail-life-limiting failure mode on modern, well-maintained high-speed track - more so than simple metal loss (wear).
Sperling ride index (Wz)A single number (typically 1–5) computed from frequency-weighted vertical or lateral car-body acceleration that predicts how a human passenger perceives the ride. Lower is smoother. It was developed by Sperling and Betzhold in 1956 for German Railways and remains the working standard in most of Europe and Asia today.
PSD (Power Spectral Density)A way of describing an irregular signal, in this case the up-and-down bumpiness of the rail surface, not as a single number but as how much "roughness energy" exists at each spatial wavelength. A rough track has more energy at short wavelengths; PSD is how track quality actually gets fed into a vibration model.
FRF (Frequency Response Function) / TransmissibilityHow much of a vibration at a given frequency gets through a spring-damper system (like the train's suspension) from the input (track roughness) to the output (car body). Below the system's natural frequency, vibration passes through almost unchanged; above it, the suspension filters it out; exactly at it, the vibration is amplified (resonance).
Natural frequency (f_n or ω_n)The frequency at which a spring-mass system (e.g. a car body sitting on its secondary air springs) will oscillate if disturbed and left alone. Suspension design is largely about placing this frequency away from the frequencies the human body is most sensitive to (4–8 Hz for vertical motion).
Damping ratio (ζ)How quickly oscillation dies out, expressed as a fraction of "critical damping" (the threshold between oscillating and not). ζ=0 means it oscillates forever once disturbed; ζ=1 means it returns to rest with no overshoot at all. Rail vehicle secondary dampers are typically tuned to ζ≈0.15–0.30 - enough to control resonance without making the ride harsh.
JerkThe rate of change of acceleration (i.e. the derivative of acceleration, third derivative of position). Acceleration itself is what pushes you back in your seat; jerk is what makes that push feel sudden or smooth. A train that reaches the same top acceleration gently (low jerk) feels far more comfortable than one that reaches it abruptly (high jerk).
Davis resistanceThe classical (1926) empirical formula for total running resistance of a rail vehicle as a function of speed, split into a constant term (rolling/bearing friction) and terms proportional to v and v² (aerodynamic drag). Still the industry-standard starting point for resistance calculations today.
Tractive effort (TE)The forward-pulling force a locomotive or powered car can actually deliver to the rail, limited by the lower of (a) what the installed motor power can produce at the current speed, and (b) what the wheel-rail adhesion limit allows before the wheel slips.
RK4 (Runge-Kutta 4th order)A numerical method for stepping a differential equation (like "velocity changes according to acceleration") forward in time in small increments, far more accurate per step than the simplest method (Euler's method) at a modest extra computational cost.

3. Purpose, Need & Benefits

Why This Platform Exists

Traditional rail engineering analysis requires expensive commercial software (SIMPACK, VAMPIRE, ADAMS/Rail) that demands specialist training and controlled environments. Engineering decisions about fleet configuration, track quality requirements, maintenance intervals, and speed upgrades are often made without accessible real-time sensitivity analysis.

This platform fills that gap by providing:

  • Instant parameter sensitivity - engineers can slide a parameter and see how braking distance, wear rate, bearing temperature, and ride quality change immediately
  • Comparative analysis - three reference datasets (Live, HSRS Baseline 160 km/h, MAHSR Target 320 km/h) visible simultaneously on all charts
  • Educational tool - formulae, standards compliance thresholds, and physical reasoning are embedded in the UI
  • No installation required - runs in any modern browser, shareable as a single HTML file

Engineering Benefits

  • Rapid assessment of speed upgrade implications (160 → 200 → 320 km/h)
  • Formation optimisation (10-car vs 16-car - tractive effort, energy, wear)
  • Track quality requirements for speed targets (Sperling index < 2.5 threshold)
  • Brake system sizing - stopping distance, disc temperature limits
  • Bearing maintenance interval planning (EN 12082 hotbox thresholds)
  • Conicity/wheel profile impact on hunting stability (Klingel wavelength)
  • MAHSR journey time estimation and ATC profile analysis

Operational Need

India's first HSR project (MAHSR, BKC–Sabarmati, 508 km, 320 km/h) requires engineering analysis of rolling stock performance that differs fundamentally from existing networks. At 320 km/h:

  • Aerodynamic drag dominates (~v³), making it 8× higher than at 160 km/h
  • Contact stress and wear rates escalate non-linearly
  • Hunting instability requires tight conicity control (< 0.05)
  • Bearing thermal loads require continuous monitoring (EN 12082)
  • Brake disc temperatures during emergency stops can exceed 400°C

Mission Raftaar - India's 160–200 km/h Upgrade Programme

Mission Raftaar was announced in the 2016–17 Railway Budget with the goal of raising average speeds for both freight and premium mail/express services, and of introducing semi-high-speed passenger operation (160–200 km/h) on selected trunk routes without waiting for dedicated new-build HSR corridors. RDSO leads the standards and track-strengthening work behind it. The programme is explicitly phased: routes are first upgraded and fenced/secured for 160 km/h running, with a further upgrade to 200 km/h following once track is strengthened with higher-grade rail (R350-class) and additional safety works (fencing, level-crossing elimination) are complete. The Delhi–Mumbai and Delhi–Howrah trunk routes have been named as priority corridors for this upgrade, alongside Delhi–Agra, Delhi–Kanpur, Chennai–Hyderabad, Nagpur–Secunderabad, Mumbai–Pune–Solapur–Hyderabad, and Mumbai–Goa as further semi-high-speed candidate routes. A 190 km stretch between Chipiyana Buzurg (Ghaziabad) and Tundla was reported as India's first section commissioned for 160 km/h running under this programme, with trial running in October 2025.

This is the direct real-world analogue of the platform's "HSRS Baseline (160 km/h)" configuration - an upgrade path for existing broad-gauge rolling stock and infrastructure, distinct from the dedicated new-build standard-gauge MAHSR corridor.

4. Software Architecture

Technology Stack

  • Three.js r128 - 3D train body / bogie visualisation with OrbitControls
  • Chart.js 3.9.1 - all 2D physics charts (animation disabled for real-time streaming)
  • Pure JavaScript - no framework; IIFE module pattern for scope isolation
  • Single HTML file - all HTML, CSS, and JS in one deployable file (~6200 lines)

Simulation Loop

The main loop runs at 100 ms intervals (10 Hz). Each tick:

  1. User inputs read from sliders → physics constants computed (computeDynamics())
  2. RK4 integration over Δt = 0.1 s → new velocity, position
  3. Brake disc temperature updated (thermal ODE)
  4. All live charts updated (Chart.js update('none') - no animation)
  5. Hook chain fired → vibration IIFE receives same dyn object → bearing temp, vibration metrics updated

Module Hook System

The main simulation loop exposes window._lapUpdateHook(dyn, now). Secondary modules (LAP Panel, Vibration IIFE) chain onto this hook so they receive live physics data without coupling to the main loop's internals.

6. Physics Engine - Core Formulae

6.1 Resistance (Davis Formula)

Davis resistance describes the total running resistance of a train as a function of speed. It captures rolling resistance, bearing drag, and aerodynamic drag.
$$ R_{total} = (A + B v)\frac{W_{total}}{1000} + W_{total}\,g\sin\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho C_D A_f v^2 $$
R_total = A + B·v [N/kN or N] where (for Indian broad gauge EMU/HSR): A = 1.5 + 0.01·W [N/kN] - rolling & bearing resistance B = 0.0065·v - aerodynamic drag coefficient term v = speed [km/h] Full resistance force [N]: F_resist = (A + B·v) × W_total [kN] / 1000 + W_total × g × sin(θ_gradient) + (aerodynamic: k_aero × v²) k_aero = 0.5 × ρ_air × C_D × A_front = 0.5 × 1.225 × 0.25 × 9.0 ≈ 1.38 [N/(m/s)²]

Why Davis?

Davis (1926) is the empirically validated standard for rail vehicle resistance. The A term captures speed-independent losses (wheel deformation, bearing seals); the B·v term captures aerodynamic skin friction and form drag. At 320 km/h the aerodynamic term dominates - it is ~4× the rolling resistance.

Effect of changing parameters on resistance:

  • Mass ↑ → A increases (heavier wheel loads), total resistance force increases proportionally
  • Speed ↑ → B·v term increases as v², aero as v³ → resistance grows rapidly
  • C_D ↑ (blunter nose) → aerodynamic resistance increases; explains why Shinkansen has long nose cones
  • Frontal area ↑ → proportional increase in aero resistance

6.2 Tractive Effort Model

$$ TE_{avail} = \min\!\left(\frac{n_{motors} P_{motor}}{v},\ \mu_{CK} W_{powered}\right), \qquad a = \frac{TE_{avail} - R_{total}}{M_{total}} $$
Available TE [N]: TE_installed = (n_motors × P_motor) / v [at speed v m/s] TE_adhesion = µ_CK × W_powered [adhesion limit] TE_available = min(TE_installed, TE_adhesion) Net acceleration: a = (TE_available - F_resist) / M_total [m/s²] RK4 integration over dt = 0.1 s: v_new = v + (k1 + 2k2 + 2k3 + k4) / 6 where k1 = a(v), k2 = a(v + k1·dt/2), etc.

Why RK4?

RK4 is 4th-order accurate: error scales as O(dt⁵) vs O(dt²) for Euler. At 10 Hz update rate (dt = 0.1 s), RK4 gives velocity accuracy better than 0.01 m/s. The additional computational cost (4 function evaluations per step) is negligible in JS.

6.3 Curtius-Kniffler Adhesion

Curtius and Kniffler published their empirical adhesion-versus-speed curve in 1950 based on measurements from German locomotives, and it was subsequently adopted as the UIC-standard reference adhesion curve for European rail traction calculations. The formula's shape, high adhesion at low speed, falling off toward a floor value at high speed, reflects real contact physics. At low speed the contact patch has time to fully "dry out" and interlock at the asperity (microscopic roughness peak) level. At high speed the contact time per revolution is too short for any surface film of moisture, oxide, or contaminant to be squeezed out, so the real area of metal-to-metal contact within the Hertzian patch shrinks.

$$ \mu_{CK}(v) = \psi \left(0.161 + \frac{7.5}{v_{kmh} + 44}\right) $$
µ_CK(v) = ψ × [0.161 + 7.5 / (v_kmh + 44)] where ψ (in-app: "Adhesion Factor", slider range 0.20–1.10, default 1.0) is a single scaling factor representing overall rail-surface condition: ψ = 1.0 → the formula reduces to the classic Curtius-Kniffler dry-rail curve ψ < 1.0 → represents reduced adhesion (moisture, leaf contamination, etc.) ψ > 1.0 → represents adhesion-enhancing conditions (sanding, up to ψ=1.1 max) At ψ=1.0, 160 km/h: µ = 1.0 × [0.161 + 7.5/204] ≈ 0.198 At ψ=1.0, 320 km/h: µ = 1.0 × [0.161 + 7.5/364] ≈ 0.182 As v → ∞: µ → 0.161·ψ (the formula's speed-independent floor) Adhesion force limit: F_adh = µ_CK × W_powered_axles [N]

Why Curtius-Kniffler (and what else exists)?

Curtius-Kniffler remains the most widely used adhesion model in European and Indian rail traction engineering because it is simple, requires no contact-patch-level detail, and its predictions have held up reasonably well against measured locomotive adhesion data for over 70 years. It is not the only model in use: Heumann's curve (1954) is a similar empirical alternative sometimes used for Japanese rolling stock calculations, and modern traction control systems increasingly rely on real-time adhesion estimation from wheel-slip sensors rather than any fixed speed-adhesion curve, precisely because actual adhesion on a given day depends heavily on weather, contamination, and leaf-fall season in ways no fixed formula captures. For a platform aimed at comparative, sensitivity-style engineering analysis rather than real-time train control, a fixed empirical curve like Curtius-Kniffler remains the right level of fidelity.

Effect of changing parameters:

  • Speed ↑ → µ decreases → adhesion limit decreases → TE_max falls → longer acceleration from high speeds
  • Adhesion factor ψ ↓ (representing wet/contaminated rail) → proportionally lower µ across all speeds → risk of wheel-slip at lower TE, and longer braking distances
  • Motor ratio ↓ → fewer powered axles → W_powered ↓ → F_adh ↓ → TE limited earlier

6.4 Emergency Braking Distance

One of the most common questions when running simulations here is: "why does the braking distance come out the same regardless of whether I run 16 cars, 8 cars, or increase the tare mass?" The short answer is that this is physically correct - not a display glitch. The longer answer requires walking through how the stopping distance is actually calculated.

The Formula

$$ d = \frac{v^2}{2\,a_{emergency}}, \qquad a_{emergency} = \frac{0.9\,\mu_{CK}\,g}{\lambda} $$
Emergency stopping distance from speed v: d = v² / (2 × a_emergency) where the available deceleration a_emergency is: a = (F_brake + R_total) / (M_total × λ) F_brake = W_total × µ_CK × 0.9 [adhesion-limited max brake force, N] W_total = M_total × g [train weight, N] λ = rotational inertia factor (1.09–1.10 for this train type) Substituting F_brake: a = (M_total × g × µ_CK × 0.9 + R_total) / (M_total × λ) ≈ g × µ_CK × 0.9 / λ (since R_total ≪ F_brake at any speed above ~30 km/h) M_total cancels from numerator and denominator.

Why the same result for 16-car, 8-car, and heavier tare?

Look at the algebra above - mass disappears from the deceleration equation entirely. A 16-car train weighs twice as much as an 8-car train, but its maximum brake force also doubles (because braking force is adhesion-limited: F_brake = weight × µ). The same kinetic energy that doubles with mass is being absorbed by a braking force that also doubled. Net result: same deceleration, same stopping distance.

This is the same physics that explains why a bicycle and a bus stop in roughly the same distance on dry roads when both are tyre-limited: mass cancels in Newton's second law whenever the applied force scales with mass. For a train using disc brakes at maximum adhesion, adding passengers or changing the consist length does not change the stopping distance at all. What does change is how much heat the brake discs absorb (more mass means more kinetic energy, which means a higher disc temperature) and how much brake pipe work is needed. The distance itself stays the same.

What speed gives ~45 m?

If you applied emergency brakes and saw roughly 45 m on the braking distance counter, the train was travelling at about 48 km/h at the moment you hit the emergency trigger. At that speed, µ_CK ≈ 0.241, giving a ≈ 1.94 m/s², and d = (48/3.6)² / (2 × 1.94) ≈ 45.7 m. That number is correct for the adhesion model in use - it just means you were at a low speed when you triggered the stop.

How the model compares against real-world references

The platform uses µ_CK × 0.9 as the effective brake coefficient - meaning it assumes the braking system delivers 90% of the theoretical adhesion maximum. In practice, emergency brake systems on high-speed trains reach roughly 50–65% of the theoretical adhesion limit because of:

  • Brake pipe pressure propagation: a pressure drop travels at ~250 m/s through the brake pipe. On a 400 m train, there is a 1–2 second delay from the leading cab to the last axle. During that time, the front brakes are fully applied but the rear ones are not yet.
  • WSP (Wheel Slide Protection) cycling: WSP releases and reapplies brakes many times per second near the adhesion limit. Average brake force is less than peak.
  • Thermal derating: brake pads lose friction coefficient as they heat up mid-stop. An emergency stop from 320 km/h raises disc temperature by 200–300°C before the train is even at half-speed.
  • Non-uniform axle loading: not all axles carry identical load, especially with dynamic weight transfer during braking. Rear bogies carry less normal force due to pitch, so their brake force contribution is lower.

The consequence is that the platform's predicted stopping distances are shorter than what a real train achieves. Here is how the model sits against recognised benchmarks:

7. Vibration Analysis

7.1 Klingel Hunting (Lateral Stability)

A railway wheelset is not two independent wheels on an axle. It is one rigid unit, and both wheels have coned (tapered) treads rather than cylindrical ones. This single design choice, dating back to the earliest railways, is what lets a wheelset self-centre on straight track and self-steer around curves without any steering mechanism at all. It is also the root cause of the platform's most important lateral-dynamics phenomenon: hunting.

Deriving the Klingel wavelength from first principles

Consider a wheelset displaced sideways by a small distance y from the track centreline. Because the treads are coned, this displacement changes the rolling radius of each wheel: the wheel that has moved toward the rail edge now rolls on a slightly larger radius, and the wheel that has moved away rolls on a slightly smaller radius. Since both wheels are rigidly connected by the same axle and must complete the same number of revolutions per second, the wheel with the larger rolling radius travels further per revolution - which steers the whole wheelset back toward the centre. It doesn't stop there, though: like a pendulum, it overshoots, and the same effect now pushes it back the other way. The result is a self-sustaining lateral oscillation as the wheelset rolls forward, first described mathematically by Klingel in 1883.

$$ \lambda_k = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{R_{wheel}\, e_{gauge}}{\lambda_{conicity}}}, \qquad f_k(v) = \frac{v}{\lambda_k} $$
Kinematic (Klingel) wavelength - purely geometric, does not depend on speed: λ_k = 2π × √(R_wheel × e_gauge / λ_conicity) where: R_wheel = nominal rolling radius [m] (= wheel diameter / 2) e_gauge = half track gauge = 0.7175 m (for 1435 mm standard gauge) λ_conicity = equivalent wheel conicity (dimensionless taper ratio) Because this is a spatial wavelength (metres per full oscillation cycle, not seconds), the oscillation FREQUENCY the wheelset experiences scales directly with how fast it is travelling: f_k(v) = v / λ_k [Hz] This is why hunting is a speed-triggered phenomenon even though its wavelength is fixed by geometry alone: the same wheelset, on the same track, simply oscillates faster (in time) as speed increases, until its oscillation frequency coincides with a structural resonance of the bogie - at which point the previously well-damped kinematic sway turns into a growing, self-reinforcing lateral oscillation.

From kinematic wavelength to a critical speed

Klingel's original result only gives the wavelength - a full dynamic critical speed additionally requires knowing how the bogie's mass, yaw inertia, and suspension stiffness respond to being driven at that wavelength-derived frequency. A rigorous answer requires solving the coupled wheelset-bogie equations of motion as an eigenvalue problem (this is what dedicated multibody codes like VAMPIRE, SIMPACK, or GENSYS actually do). This platform instead uses a single-degree-of-freedom resonance heuristic: it treats the bogie's primary-suspension yaw stiffness and the wheelset/bogie yaw moment of inertia as a torsional oscillator, and estimates the critical speed as the point where the Klingel kinematic frequency matches that torsional system's own natural frequency.

$$ f_{yaw} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k_{yaw}}{I_{bogie}}}, \qquad V_c = \lambda_k \cdot f_{yaw} $$
In-app critical hunting speed (index.html, computeConstants()): Bogie yaw natural frequency: f_yaw = (1/2π) × √(k_yaw / I_bogie) k_yaw = user-set yaw (primary suspension) stiffness [Nm/rad] I_bogie = 1800 kg·m² (fixed assumed bogie yaw moment of inertia) Critical speed: Vc = λ_k × f_yaw [m/s] (Klingel wavelength × yaw resonance frequency)

Why Does Conicity Matter?

Wheel conicity is the primary design parameter controlling hunting stability, and it appears under the square root in the denominator of the wavelength formula - so λ_k shrinks as conicity rises, which in turn lowers the critical speed Vc. New wheel profiles are cut with a low, controlled conicity; as a wheel wears in service, its effective conicity typically increases (the tread hollows out and the effective rolling-radius-difference-per-lateral-displacement grows), which is why hunting risk grows over a wheelset's service life even though nothing about the bogie itself has changed. When train speed approaches Vc, the bogie enters sustained lateral oscillation (hunting), causing:

  • Increased lateral acceleration (passenger discomfort, and eventually a measurable safety concern under EN 14363's ΣY/Q derailment criteria)
  • Flange contact against the rail gauge face on every oscillation cycle → accelerated gauge-face wear → corrugation risk
  • Derailment risk if conicity is very high and operating speed is near or above Vc

HSR rule of thumb: maintain equivalent conicity < 0.05 for 320 km/h operation, with wheel reprofiling triggered well before conicity exceeds 0.1 - reprofiling intervals are set specifically to keep conicity within this safe band throughout the wheel's service life, not just when new.

India-specific note: two gauges, one platform. Indian Railways' existing network runs almost entirely on 1,676 mm broad gauge (half-gauge e = 0.838 m), among the widest in-service gauges in the world, chosen in the 19th century partly for stability and capacity reasons. The Mumbai–Ahmedabad High Speed Rail (MAHSR) corridor that this platform's "MAHSR Target" reference configuration represents is being built to 1,435 mm standard gauge instead (half-gauge e = 0.7175 m), specifically because it uses Shinkansen E5-derived technology sourced from Japan, which is standard-gauge throughout. This platform's hunting/Klingel calculation uses the standard-gauge value (0.7175 m) for both the "HSRS Baseline" and "MAHSR Target" reference configurations. That is correct for the MAHSR case, but it is a known simplification for the baseline case, which represents a broad-gauge-style semi-high-speed EMU physically while computing its hunting dynamics as if it were standard gauge. Because the Klingel wavelength scales only as the square root of gauge (λ_k ∝ √e), the resulting difference is modest: using the real broad-gauge value would increase λ_k, and therefore Vc, by a factor of √(0.838/0.7175) ≈ 1.08, roughly 8% higher than shown. Better to say so plainly than leave it as an unstated approximation.

7.3 Sperling Ride Index (Wz)

Sperling and Betzhold's 1956 ride index remains the working comfort metric across most of Europe and Asia (it underlies DIN 45673, and is referenced by UIC 513 and, in India, RDSO ride-quality assessments) precisely because it compresses a whole spectrum of vibration into one number a maintenance engineer can act on. The core insight is that the human body is not equally annoyed by vibration at every frequency: a jolt at 5 Hz feels far worse than the same acceleration amplitude at 20 Hz. So the formula weights each frequency band by an empirically-derived annoyance curve before combining them.

$$ W_{z,i} = 0.896\,\big(a_i \cdot B(f_i)\big)^{0.3}, \qquad W_z = \left(\sum_i W_{z,i}^{10}\right)^{0.1} $$
Per-frequency-band Sperling contribution: $$ W_{z,i} = 0.896 \times \big(a_i \cdot B(f_i)\big)^{0.3} $$ a_i = peak acceleration in that narrow frequency band [cm/s²] B(f_i) = frequency weighting factor for that band (rises 0.5–5 Hz, flat 5–6 Hz at the most sensitive point, falls above 6 Hz) Combining all bands (10th-power / Minkowski summation - a small number of moderately bad bands is far less punishing than one very bad band, matching how a single dominant resonance dominates perceived ride quality in practice): $$ W_z = \left( \sum_i W_{z,i}^{\,10} \right)^{0.1} $$ Interpretation: Wz ≤ 2.5 - Excellent (imperceptible motion) 2.5–3.0 - Good (just perceptible) 3.0–3.5 - Acceptable (clearly perceptible) 3.5–4.5 - Poor (strongly perceptible) > 4.5 - Very poor (dangerous)

8.1 Brake Disc Temperature

$$ \frac{dT}{dt} = \frac{Q_{in} - Q_{cool}}{m_{disc}\,c_p}, \qquad Q_{in} = F_{brake}\,v,\qquad Q_{cool} = h_{conv} A_{disc}(T - T_\infty) $$
Brake disc thermal ODE: dT/dt = (Q_in - Q_cool) / (m_disc × c_p) Heat input during braking: Q_in = F_brake × v [W] (braking power) F_brake = µ_brake × N_normal × n_discs = brake_ratio × F_adhesion_limit × efficiency Cooling (convective, both faces): Q_cool = h_conv × A_disc × (T - T_ambient) h_conv = 15 + 1.5 × v_ms [W/m²K] (forced convection at speed v) A_disc = 0.2 m² (effective cooling area per disc) m_disc = 15 kg (cast iron disc mass) c_p = 500 J/kgK (cast iron specific heat) Ambient temperature T_ambient = 30°C (Indian summer baseline)

Why Do Discs Get So Hot?

Braking power Q_in = F × v is very large at high speed. For an emergency stop from 160 km/h (44.4 m/s) with a 16-car train (624 t): F_brake ≈ 400 kN → Q_in ≈ 17.8 MW split across 64 discs → 278 kW per disc. This raises temperature at ~278,000 / (15 × 500) = 37°C/s. Even a 10-second high-speed braking phase raises temperature by ~370°C.

Natural Cooling

At rest (v=0): h_conv = 15 W/m²K, Q_cool = 15 × 0.2 × (T-30) = 3(T-30) W. From 400°C: dT/dt = -3×370/(15×500) = -0.15°C/s → 41 minutes to cool from 400°C to 50°C. This is realistic for cast iron brake discs - they cool slowly at rest, which is why the Reset button exists for fresh test scenarios.

Parameter Effects:

  • Speed ↑ → Q_in increases proportionally; cooling increases too (h_conv ↑) but Q_in grows faster → higher peak temperature
  • Brake ratio ↑ → F_brake ↑ → Q_in ↑ → higher disc temp
  • Emergency mode → maximum F_brake → highest disc temperature (can exceed 500°C from 320 km/h)
  • More discs (n_discs ↑) → heat shared across more discs → lower temperature per disc

8.2 Bearing Temperature (EN 12082)

$$ T_{bearing} = T_\infty + \mu_{bearing}\,F_{axle}\,v\,R_{eff}, \qquad R_{eff}=\frac{0.085}{1+0.001v} $$
Bearing heat generation: P_bearing = µ_bearing × F_axle × v [W] where: µ_bearing = 0.0001 (rolling bearing overall friction incl. seals, lubrication drag) F_axle = W_total / (n_cars × 4 × n_axles_per_bogie × 2) per bearing ≈ total weight / total number of bearings v = speed [m/s] Effective thermal resistance: R_eff = 0.085 / (1 + 0.001 × v) [K/W] Steady-state bearing temperature: T_bearing = T_ambient + P_bearing × R_eff = 30 + µ_bearing × F_axle × v × R_eff Typical values: 160 km/h: ΔT ≈ 18–22°C above ambient → T ≈ 48–52°C (Normal) 320 km/h: ΔT ≈ 30–40°C above ambient → T ≈ 60–70°C (Normal/Watch) EN 12082 Hotbox Thresholds: Alarm: ΔT > 50K OR T > 80°C Danger: ΔT > 80K OR T > 110°C

Why µ_bearing = 0.0001?

A precision CARB/TBU (tapered roller) bearing for axleboxes has rolling contact friction µ ≈ 0.0005–0.001. However, the effective coefficient including viscous drag from grease, seal friction, and gear side loads, at operating temperatures where grease is fully fluid, falls to µ_eff ≈ 0.0001–0.0003. The value 0.0001 corresponds to a well-maintained bearing at operating temperature - conservative but realistic for simulation purposes. It correctly produces ΔT ≈ 20°C at 160 km/h, consistent with EN 12082 normal class.

9. Rail-Wheel Tribology

This is the section that governs the platform's Contact Mechanics and Rail-Wheel Lab tabs: 17 live charts, plus live numeric readouts for contact geometry, shakedown state, and wear regime. It is also the section most people ask about, because unlike braking or thermal behaviour, wheel-rail contact mechanics is not something most engineers encounter in general mechanical or civil curricula. What follows walks through the theory from first principles, states exactly what the platform computes, and is explicit about where the in-app formula is an exact textbook result versus an engineering approximation.

9.1 Hertz Contact Theory

In 1882, Heinrich Hertz, better known today for his work on electromagnetic waves, published the first rigorous solution for the stress field produced when two curved elastic bodies are pressed together. A wheel rolling on a rail is exactly this problem: a convex wheel tread, curved in two directions (around its circumference, and across its slightly conical profile), presses onto a convex rail head, curved across its rounded crown but essentially flat along its length. Hertz showed that under three assumptions, that both surfaces are smooth and continuous near the contact point, that both materials are linearly elastic, and that the contact patch is small compared to the radii of curvature involved, the resulting contact patch is an ellipse, and the pressure distribution over that ellipse takes the form of a perfect three-dimensional dome (a semi-ellipsoid).

$$ a = m_H\sqrt[3]{\frac{3F}{4E^*(A+B)}},\quad b = n_H\sqrt[3]{\frac{3F}{4E^*(A+B)}},\qquad p_0 = \frac{3F}{2\pi a b} $$
Step 1 - Curvatures at the contact point: Rolling-direction curvature (wheel only, rail is ~flat lengthwise): 1/Rx = 1/R_wheel Transverse (cross-track) curvature (wheel tread crown + rail head crown, both convex): 1/Ry = 1/R_wheel,transverse + 1/R_rail,crown Curvature sum: A + B = ½(1/Rx + 1/Ry) Curvature ratio: k = (1/Ry) / (1/Rx) - this determines how elongated the ellipse is Step 2 - Contact ellipse semi-axes: a = m_H × c (semi-axis ALONG the rail - the long way, rolling direction) b = n_H × c (semi-axis ACROSS the rail - the short way, transverse) c = ∛[ 3F / (4E* (A+B)) ] m_H, n_H = dimensionless Hertz/Timoshenko coefficients, functions of k (see table below - this is the one place the closed-form Hertz solution requires either a table lookup or an approximation) Combined (reduced) elastic modulus: 1/E* = ½[(1-ν_w²)/E_w + (1-ν_r²)/E_r] For steel wheel on steel rail (E=210 GPa, ν=0.3 both): E* ≈ 115.4 GPa Step 3 - Peak Hertzian pressure (at the centre of the ellipse): p₀ = 3F / (2π·a·b) [Pa] The pressure distribution is semi-ellipsoidal: p(x,y) = p₀·√[1-(x/a)²-(y/b)²] Average pressure over the patch = (2/3)·p₀ - this is why p₀, not the average, is what matters for yield and fatigue: the material at the very centre of the patch sees 1.5× the average stress. Contact area: A_contact = π·a·b Normal force per wheel: F = W_total / (n_axles × 2)

At θ=90° (equal curvature in both directions, i.e. a spherical contact like a ball bearing) the ellipse degenerates to a circle and m_H=n_H=1 - a useful sanity check. As θ decreases, the two surfaces' curvatures diverge more, and the contact patch stretches into a longer, thinner ellipse.

Because looking up an elliptic-integral table on every simulation frame is impractical in a browser running at 10 Hz, this platform uses a closed-form approximation instead of the table:

$$ m \approx 1 + 0.6\ln k, \qquad n \approx 1 - 0.2\ln k $$
In-app approximation (index.html, computeConstants()): m ≈ 1 + 0.6 × ln(k) n ≈ 1 - 0.2 × ln(k) where k = curvature ratio (1/Ry)/(1/Rx)

This is not the textbook Hertz/Timoshenko table, and it is not the other commonly-cited closed-form alternative in the literature, the Brewe-Hamrock (1977) ellipticity approximation κ ≈ (B/A)^(2/π), which was derived by least-squares regression against the exact elliptic-integral solution and is accurate to within about 3%. The logarithmic form used here is a simpler engineering approximation. It was checked against the exact behaviour at the two points that matter most for correctness:

  • Boundary condition: at k=1 (equal curvature, circular contact) it correctly gives m=n=1, matching Hertz exactly.
  • Typical HSR operating point: for a 915 mm wheel with a 500 mm transverse crown radius on a 300 mm rail head crown (k ≈ 2.45), the approximation gives m≈1.54, n≈0.82, i.e. an ellipse with b/a ≈ 0.53 - a roughly 2:1 aspect ratio contact patch. Measured wheel-rail contact patches on new profiles are typically reported in the range 8–15 mm long by 5–10 mm wide (a 1.5:1 to 2.5:1 ratio), so the approximation's shape is physically reasonable, even though it is not the exact Hertz value.

Running the platform's live contact-mechanics readout with default parameters gives a=6.9 mm, b=3.8 mm, p₀=1034 MPa - well within the range independently reported for new high-speed wheel/rail profiles (typically 800–1400 MPa for HSR axle loads of 10–14 t per axle), which is a reasonable confidence check on the overall approach even though the m/n coefficient formula itself is a simplification rather than the literature-exact value.

Why does p₀ matter so much?

Peak Hertzian pressure is the single number that determines whether the wheel-rail contact stays in the elastic regime or starts accumulating permanent damage. Section 10 (Rolling Contact Fatigue) below covers this in depth, but the short version: below roughly 2× the material's yield strength, only elastic deformation happens and the material recovers fully after each wheel pass; above that threshold, the material starts to permanently deform a little on each pass, which eventually produces surface cracking. For R350HT rail steel (yield strength ≥ 880 MPa), that threshold sits in the 1500–1800 MPa range - comfortably above the platform's typical computed p₀ of ~1000–1400 MPa for HSR loads, but close enough that axle load and wheel diameter choices genuinely matter.

9.2 Creepage - What "Slip" Actually Means in Rolling Contact

Before getting to the Polach model, it is worth being precise about creepage, because the word "slip" is misleading. A powered wheel under traction is not spinning faster than the train is moving in the way a car tyre spins on ice - the difference is measured in fractions of a percent, not tens of percent. The contact patch is elastic: near the leading edge it sticks fully to the rail (no relative motion), and only near the trailing edge does genuine micro-slip occur, growing as the tangential force demanded gets closer to the adhesion limit.

$$ \xi_x = \frac{v_{wheel} - v_{rail}}{v_{rail}}, \qquad \xi_y = \sin\psi + \frac{v_{lat}}{v}, \qquad \xi_{sp} = \frac{\omega\sin\delta}{v} $$
Longitudinal creepage (traction/braking direction): ξ_x = (v_wheel_surface - v_rail) / v_rail where v_wheel_surface = ω_wheel × R_rolling (angular speed × rolling radius) and v_rail is the train's actual translational speed. Positive ξ_x → wheel surface moving faster than train → traction (driving) Negative ξ_x → wheel surface moving slower than train → braking Lateral creepage (from yaw / hunting motion, or curve negotiation): ξ_y = sin(ψ) + (lateral velocity component) / v where ψ = the wheelset's yaw angle relative to the track centreline Spin creepage (from the contact patch not being perpendicular to the axle, due to conicity and the wheel/rail contact angle): ξ_sp = ω_wheel × sin(δ) / v where δ = contact angle (related to conicity and lateral wheelset position)

The platform's live telemetry uses a representative fixed working-point creepage (0.3%, i.e. ξ=0.003) to compute T-γ and wear on the live charts - this value sits deliberately in the middle of the typical steady-state traction/braking creepage range (0.1–0.5%) rather than being derived frame-by-frame from wheel slip dynamics, which would require modelling individual wheelset yaw and torsional dynamics that are out of scope for a vehicle-level simulator. The Rail-Wheel Lab's creep-force-vs-creepage chart (Chart ④) does sweep the full creepage range from 0% to 10%, which is where the saturation behaviour described below actually becomes visible.

9.3 The Polach Tangential Contact Model

Once the normal contact solution (Hertz) and the creepage are known, the next question is: how much tangential (traction/braking) force can actually be transmitted through that creepage? The first rigorous answer came from Joost Kalker's linear theory (1967), which relates small creepages linearly to tangential force through a set of dimensionless coefficients, universally called the Kalker coefficients C₁₁ (longitudinal), C₂₂ (lateral), and C₂₃ (spin-lateral coupling). Kalker tabulated these as functions of the contact ellipse aspect ratio b/a and Poisson's ratio - for a typical wheel-rail ellipticity around b/a≈0.5 and ν=0.28 (steel), published tables give C₁₁ in roughly the 4–5 range.

$$ F_{T,linear} = -C_{11}\,G\,a\,b\,\xi $$
Kalker linear theory (valid only for very small creepage, ξ ≲ 0.1%): F_T,linear = -C₁₁ × G × a × b × ξ where G = combined shear modulus ≈ 80.77 GPa for steel/steel Problem: this grows without bound as ξ increases - physically impossible, since the tangential force cannot exceed the adhesion limit µN.

Kalker's linear theory breaks down almost immediately in practice, because real traction and braking creepages regularly exceed 0.1%. Oldrich Polach's 2000 model ("A Fast Wheel-Rail Forces Calculation Computer Code") solves this by introducing a saturation function that smoothly bends the linear relationship over into the correct large-slip asymptote (F_T → µN as ξ → ∞), while still recovering Kalker's linear result for very small ξ:

$$ F_T = \frac{2\mu N}{\pi}\left[\frac{\varepsilon}{1+\varepsilon^2} + \arctan\varepsilon\right], \qquad \varepsilon = \frac{C_{11}\,G\,\pi\,a\,b\,\xi}{4\mu N} $$
Polach tangential force: F_T = (2µN/π) × [ ε/(1+ε²) + arctan(ε) ] where the "reduced creepage" ε absorbs all the Kalker/Hertz geometry: ε = (C₁₁ × G × π × a × b × ξ) / (4 × µ × N) As ξ → 0: ε → 0, and F_T → (2µN/π)×[ε + ε] = (4µN/π)×ε (recovers linear regime) As ξ → ∞: ε → ∞, and F_T → (2µN/π)×[0 + π/2] = µN (correctly saturates) In-app simplification (index.html, buildRWCharts() → polachF()): ε = (π × C_11 × ξ × a × b) / (4 × µ × N) with C_11 = 4.78 (fixed, representative of b/a≈0.5, ν≈0.28)

The in-app value C₁₁=4.78 is a single fixed representative value rather than a live lookup against the current contact ellipse's actual b/a ratio, which does shift slightly as the wheel diameter and axle load sliders move. This is a reasonable simplification, since C₁₁ varies only modestly (roughly ±20%) over the range of b/a ratios realistic for HSR wheel/rail pairs. It does mean the creep-force curve will not shift noticeably when, say, the wheel diameter slider is moved, even though in reality it would shift a little.

Why the "live" creep force curve can sit above its own reference lines: The Rail-Wheel Lab's Chart ④ (Polach Creep Curve) plots the creep force computed at the current live simulation speed, while its two dashed reference lines ("VB µ×N", "MAHSR µ×N") are drawn at fixed reference speeds of 160 km/h and 320 km/h. Because adhesion µ rises sharply as speed falls (Curtius-Kniffler, §6.3), a stationary or slow-moving train has a much higher µ than either reference point - so immediately after loading the page (train stationary), the live curve's saturation plateau will sit visibly above both reference lines. This is expected, physically correct behaviour, not a bug: start the simulation and bring the train up to 160 or 320 km/h, and the live curve converges toward the corresponding reference line.

9.4 Archard Wear Model

John Archard's 1953 wear equation is the standard starting point for predicting how much material is removed from two surfaces in sliding contact. It says, in effect, that the volume of debris produced is proportional to how hard the surfaces are pressed together, how far they slide relative to each other, and inversely proportional to how hard the material is - with all the messy, hard-to-predict physics of asperity contact, adhesion, and debris formation folded into a single empirical dimensionless coefficient, K.

$$ V_{wear} = \frac{K\, F_N\, s}{H}, \qquad s = \xi \cdot d $$
Archard wear volume: V_wear = K × F_N × s / H where: K = dimensionless wear coefficient (see regimes below) F_N = normal contact force [N] s = sliding (relative slip) distance [m] H = material hardness expressed as a pressure [Pa] (H ≈ 2.8–3.0 × HB × 9.81×10⁶, roughly; R350HT rail steel at 350-390 HB gives H ≈ 2.5 GPa) Sliding distance per km of travel: s = ξ × d, d = 1000 m per km travelled Output: mm³ of material removed per km of travel, per wheel

The wear coefficient K - and why "one number" is a simplification

Archard's original work on dry sliding metal contacts found K spanning roughly six orders of magnitude - from K≈10⁻⁸ for genuinely mild, oxidative wear up to K≈10⁻² for severe adhesive (galling) wear. Rail-specific research narrows this considerably because wheel-rail contact is rolling-sliding rather than pure sliding, and because the relevant regimes for steel-on-steel rail contact have been characterised experimentally using twin-disc rigs. The platform uses a two-value switch:

In-app K values (index.html, wearAt()): K = 1×10⁻⁴ for T-γ/A below the mild/severe threshold K = 3×10⁻⁴ for T-γ/A above it (more severe sliding-dominated wear)

This two-value approach is a simplification of a continuum. The most widely cited experimental classification for rail-specific wear regimes is Lewis & Dwyer-Joyce's twin-disc wear map (University of Sheffield), which plots measured wear rate (µg per metre rolled per mm² of contact area) against the wear number Tγ/A (frictional work rate normalised by contact area, in N/mm²):

Severe (adhesive/delamination)10.4 – 77.2 N/mm²1–10 µg/mThe oxide film cannot form fast enough to protect the surface; metal-to-metal adhesive wear and sub-surface plastic flow dominate. Wear rate jumps roughly an order of magnitude at this transition.

This table (also shown in the platform's Wear & Fatigue tab) is what the app's own hardcoded switch threshold is loosely modelled on. It is worth being precise about units here, because it is exactly the kind of mismatch that is easy to miss: the Lewis-Olofsson Tγ/A index is normalised by contact area A (giving N/mm², a pressure-like quantity), whereas the raw "T-γ" figure fed into the Archard wear-volume calculation is not - it is simply tangential force times creepage, in newtons, and dividing by contact area is a separate step. The Contact Mechanics tab now performs that division itself and shows both numbers side by side: the raw T-γ (N) used internally by the Archard formula, and the area-normalised Tγ/A (N/mm²) that the Mild / Severe / Catastrophic classification above is actually based on, with the live classification displayed directly rather than left for the user to work out.

Parameter effects on wear:

  • Speed ↑ → adhesion µ falls (Curtius-Kniffler) but required tractive/braking force stays similar, so ε and creep force can rise; more significantly, higher speed increases sliding distance per unit time
  • Conicity ↑ → more lateral hunting motion → lateral creepage ξ_y increases → wear rate rises sharply as the train approaches its critical hunting speed
  • Axle load ↑ → normal force F_N rises → both p₀ (Hertz) and wear volume increase - wear volume scales roughly linearly with load, while p₀ scales with the cube root of load, which is why axle load limits are set by fatigue (p₀) long before they are set by simple wear rate
  • Rail/wheel hardness ↑ → H increases in the denominator → wear volume falls proportionally; this is the entire engineering rationale for head-hardened rail (R350HT, 350–390 HB) over standard-strength rail (R260, ~260 HB) on high-speed lines

10. Rolling Contact Fatigue & the Shakedown Map

Wear (material physically removed) and rolling contact fatigue (cracks forming beneath or at the surface without necessarily removing material) are two different failure modes, and on modern hardened rail steel run at controlled speeds, RCF is usually the one that actually limits rail and wheel life - not simple wear. This section did not previously exist in the documentation; it is added here because the platform's Wear & Fatigue tab already displays a shakedown map and RCF-related wear mechanisms, but the theory behind them was never explained.

10.1 The Shakedown Map

When a rolling contact load passes over a point on the rail repeatedly, the material's response depends on how the peak contact stress compares to the material's yield strength. This was formalised by K.L. Johnson (1989) into what is now called the shakedown map:

Shakedown regimes, by peak contact pressure p₀ relative to yield strength σ_y: p₀ < ~2.5σ_y (approx.) → Purely elastic - no permanent deformation at all ~2.5σ_y < p₀ < ~4σ_y (approx.) → Elastic shakedown - a small amount of plastic strain occurs on the very first few passes, then the material "shakes down" to a stable state where all subsequent cycles are purely elastic (a protective residual stress field has built up) p₀ > ~4σ_y (approx.) → Plastic shakedown / ratchetting - every pass adds a small increment of permanent plastic strain in the same direction; this accumulates without limit and is the root cause of most RCF cracking

For R350HT rail steel (yield strength ≥ 880 MPa), this puts the elastic/shakedown boundary in roughly the 1500–1800 MPa p₀ range and the shakedown/ratchetting boundary around 2200–2400 MPa. That is broadly consistent with the platform's own Wear & Fatigue tab, which shows an elastic regime below 900 MPa, elastic shakedown 900–1558 MPa, plastic shakedown 1558–2200 MPa, and ratchetting above 2200 MPa. The platform's numbers are somewhat more conservative than the generic Johnson multiples above, which makes sense: real rail steel shakedown limits depend on details like residual stress from head-hardening, and are usually taken from measured or standard-specific data such as EN 13674-1's fatigue design limits rather than derived purely from a generic multiple of yield strength.

The key practical point: a wheel-rail contact operating in the elastic shakedown regime is not damage-free forever - the "shakedown" is only stable if nothing else changes. Thermal loading (a hot day, or heat from nearby braking), a slightly out-of-round wheel, or a rail joint impact can transiently push the local contact stress well above the nominal p₀, triggering a ratchetting increment even though the nominal, steady-state operating point looks safely in the shakedown zone. This is why RCF inspection intervals matter even on well-designed, apparently comfortably-loaded high-speed lines.

Why HSR track sees a different RCF profile than freight or classical mainline

Two things shift the RCF picture at high speed compared to a conventional 100–120 km/h railway. First, axle loads on high-speed EMU and trainset stock are relatively light, typically 14–17 t/axle against up to 25 t/axle for freight in India, which keeps p₀ comfortably inside the shakedown envelope under normal conditions. High-speed lines are generally not wear-limited the way heavy-haul freight lines are. Second, the tight conicity control required for hunting stability at 300+ km/h (§7.1, conicity < 0.05) keeps the contact patch running in a narrow, repeatable band on both the wheel tread and rail head. That is good for predictability, but it also means that band sees a very high number of load cycles at nearly the same location, favouring fatigue (squats, head checks) over general wear as the dominant degradation mode. This is why RDSO and international HSR maintenance regimes for lines like MAHSR emphasise ultrasonic rail flaw detection and periodic wheel/rail profile grinding, removing a thin RCF-affected surface layer before cracks can propagate, as much as, or more than, simple wear-driven reprofiling.

11. Ride Comfort & Suspension FRF

11.1 Suspension Frequency Response Function

$$ |H(f)| = \sqrt{\frac{1+(2\zeta r)^2}{(1-r^2)^2+(2\zeta r)^2}}, \quad r=\frac{f}{f_n},\ f_n=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{k_s}{m_{car}}} $$
Transmissibility (FRF) of secondary suspension: |H(f)| = √[(1 + (2ζr)²) / ((1 - r²)² + (2ζr)²)] where: r = f / f_n (frequency ratio) f_n = (1/2π) × √(k_s / m_car) [Hz] (natural frequency) ζ = c / (2√(k_s × m_car)) (damping ratio) In decibels: |H(f)|_dB = 20 × log₁₀(|H(f)|) Interpretation: |H| < 0 dB: attenuation (isolation) - above √2 × f_n |H| > 0 dB: amplification - near resonance (f ≈ f_n) |H| at f_n = 1/(2ζ) for underdamped system

Design Implications:

  • Target f_n ≈ 1.0–1.5 Hz for secondary suspension (below human sensitivity peak at 4–8 Hz)
  • At 0.25 damping: resonance peak ≈ 2× amplification, good isolation above 2 Hz
  • Softer springs (lower k_s): better isolation but more body sway → coupling with lateral dynamics
  • Harder springs: limits sway but transmits vibration more → worse Sperling Wz on rough track
